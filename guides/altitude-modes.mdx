Helix supports three altitude modes for crewed aircraft missions. All
three use the same planning pipeline but build the flight profile
differently.

| Mode   | Per-strip altitude | Terrain-following | Waypoint density |
| ------ | ------------------ | ----------------- | ---------------- |
| AMSL   | One flat MSL value for the whole plan | No | Endpoints only |
| iAMSL  | One optimal flat MSL value **per strip** | Statistical | Endpoints only |
| iAGL   | Dense variable-altitude profile per strip | Predictive | 2 to 1000 waypoints |

The drone-only `AGL` mode is reserved for future use — use iAGL or
iAMSL for airplane/helicopter missions.

## AMSL — flat altitude above mean sea level

The simplest mode: every strip flies at the entered MSL altitude
exactly. The only terrain interaction happens before GSD is computed,
where the planner samples the AOI bounding box on a 20x20 grid and
subtracts the **maximum** terrain elevation from the MSL altitude to
obtain the *effective above-ground height* that feeds the sensor math.
This prevents GSD from being wildly overestimated on elevated terrain.

AMSL is the right choice over flat country, over water, or when external
regulatory constraints fix a specific MSL corridor.

<Note>
  When leading lines are enabled and a terrain provider is available,
  even AMSL strips get terrain-aware approach/departure extensions — see
  [Leading lines](/guides/leading-lines).
</Note>

## iAMSL — one optimal flat altitude per strip

iAMSL (intelligent AMSL) computes a **single** best-fit MSL altitude for
each strip independently. The strip body itself stays flat, but different
strips can sit at different altitudes, so a mission over rolling terrain no
longer has to climb to clear the worst point everywhere.

The per-strip altitude is derived in four steps:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Sample the centerline">
    The strip is divided into `n` samples between its start and end,
    where `n = clamp(stripLength / step + 1, 2, 1000)`. The sample step
    is the same auto-computed step used by iAGL (see below) — four
    samples per second of flight, clamped to `[2, 100]` m.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Apply the tolerance filter">
    Consecutive elevation deltas smaller than the terrain tolerance
    (default 1 m) are snapped to the previous sample, so the profile
    ignores sub-tolerance DEM noise.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Take the median">
    The filtered terrain samples are sorted and the **median** is used as
    the reference terrain elevation. Median is robust to single outliers
    (one tall tree, one pit), unlike mean or max.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add the flight height">
    The final per-strip altitude is
    `flight_altitude_msl = median_terrain_msl + flight_height_agl`.
  </Step>
</Steps>

The result is a single altitude per strip: the strip body stays flat at
that altitude, and downstream statistics and reports use the same value.

<Tip>
  iAMSL minimises AGL variance across a strip without giving up the
  aerodynamic and photogrammetric benefits of level flight. It's the
  default choice for gentle rolling terrain where a full iAGL profile
  would just add waypoint noise with no real coverage gain.
</Tip>

## iAGL — dense terrain-following with physical clamp

iAGL is the heavy-weight mode. Each strip becomes a **dense polyline**
of waypoints that hugs the terrain at the configured flight height AGL,
with the guarantee that no two consecutive waypoints ever violate the
aircraft's maximum climb or descent rate. No spline, no smoothing —
the guarantee is mathematical.

Here is what happens per strip, in order:

### 1. Auto sample step and lookahead

Both parameters are derived from ground speed; the user does **not**
override them (locked by product spec).

```text
sample_step (m) = clamp( speed_ms * 0.25, 2.0, 100.0 )   # 4 samples/sec
lookahead   (m) = speed_ms * ( 50 / max_climb_rate_ms + 5 )
```

The `50 / maxClimbRate` term means slow-climbing aircraft start reacting
to terrain earlier; the constant `+5` seconds is pilot reaction + control
lag.

### 2. Sample, lookahead, tolerance

<Steps>
  <Step title="Sample the terrain">
    `n = clamp(stripLength / step + 1, 2, 1000)` waypoint positions are
    placed uniformly between the strip's start and end. The DEM is
    queried once per position.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Forward lookahead">
    For each sample, the terrain value is replaced by the **max** of
    `terrain[w .. w + lookahead_steps]`. This is what makes the profile
    "know about" rising ground ahead before it actually gets there.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Tolerance snap">
    Just like iAMSL, consecutive deltas smaller than the terrain
    tolerance are snapped so the aircraft doesn't chase sub-metre DEM
    noise.
  </Step>
</Steps>

After these three passes the per-waypoint safety floor is
`raw_alts[w] = filtered_terrain[w] + flight_height_agl` — the final
profile must never descend below it.

### 3. Fixed-point forward / backward clamp

The physically-feasible profile is obtained by iterating two passes until
convergence (practically 2–3 iterations on worst-case terrain, capped at
8):

```text
# segment dt and per-segment vertical budgets
dt              = seg_len / speed_ms
max_climb_seg   = max_climb_rate_ms * dt
max_desc_seg    = max_descent_rate_ms * dt

# Forward pass (i-1 -> i)
for i in 1..n-1:
    floor_desc = profile[i-1] - max_desc_seg
    ceil_climb = profile[i-1] + max_climb_seg
    profile[i] = clamp(profile[i], floor_desc, ceil_climb)
    profile[i] = max(profile[i], raw_alts[i])        # terrain floor wins

# Backward pass (i+1 -> i)
for i in n-2..0:
    required  = profile[i+1] - max_climb_seg          # must be able to reach i+1
    profile[i] = max(profile[i], required)
    profile[i] = max(profile[i], raw_alts[i])         # terrain floor wins
```

The two passes are intentionally asymmetric:

- **Forward pass** clamps the *current* sample by physics from the
  previous sample (can't climb or descend faster than the rate allows),
  then re-floors to the terrain-safety floor.
- **Backward pass** looks at the *next* sample and pushes the current one
  up if it isn't high enough for the aircraft to reach `profile[i+1]` in
  one segment. This is what guarantees the profile climbs **in advance**
  of rising terrain instead of hitting a wall.

<Warning>
  The terrain floor `raw_alts[w]` always wins after each clamp. There is
  no scenario where the feasible profile dips below the safety floor —
  if the aircraft's climb rate is insufficient to clear an obstacle, the
  backward pass will raise the entire leading ramp until it can.
</Warning>

The loop stops when `max_delta < 1e-6` between two iterations, or after
8 iterations as a safety cap. In every practical case convergence
happens in 2 or 3 passes.

### 4. Output

Each waypoint carries its position, MSL altitude and terrain MSL and
is attached to the strip. In iAGL mode the strip body has no single
altitude — its profile lives entirely in the waypoint list. The
planner also stores a per-strip terrain profile used by the statistics
and reports.

## Leading lines under each mode

Leading lines (approach/departure extensions) are attached after the
strip body is computed, regardless of mode. Their semantics are covered
in detail in [Leading lines](/guides/leading-lines), but the short
version is:

- **AMSL**: leading lines extend at the strip's flat MSL altitude and only
  climb when the terrain safety floor rises above it.
- **iAMSL**: same, but the junction altitude is the strip's
  per-strip-optimised MSL.
- **iAGL**: the junction altitude is the first/last body waypoint, so the
  leading line smoothly picks up from wherever the terrain-following
  profile ended.

In all three cases the climb and descent rates are respected exactly the
same way, using the same fixed-point clamp described above.
