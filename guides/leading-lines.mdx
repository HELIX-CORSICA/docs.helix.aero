title: "Leading lines"
description: "Level-flight approach and departure extensions: junction-locked profile, climb-only terrain avoidance, and how the engine guarantees the aircraft returns to zero attitude as soon as possible."
---

A **leading line** is the flat extension attached at each end of a flight
strip. It gives the aircraft a stabilisation runway to enter and exit the
band at level attitude — cameras can't capture usable imagery while the
aircraft is still settling from a turn or a climb, so you need a clean
straight-in segment of known altitude at both sides of every strip.

Leading lines are attached automatically by the planner after strip
routing, using a level-flight profile that obeys the semantic rule
below.

## The semantic rule

The product spec for the leading-line profile is literal:

> **"Dès la fin de bande, rétablir une assiette nulle, progressivement et
> conformément à la pente max possible par l'appareil. Pour l'AMSL
> simple : prolonger la bande au même Z. Pas d'AGL de redescente sur la
> leading line — AGL de montée uniquement (esquive sol) — avec pour
> objectif de stabiliser l'assiette à nulle dès que possible."**

Translated:

- As soon as the strip ends, the aircraft must return to **level
  attitude** as fast as the climb/descent rates allow.
- In plain AMSL mode the line extends at exactly the strip's altitude.
- The leading line **never descends below the junction altitude to follow
  a valley** — AGL only comes into play as a climb floor for terrain
  avoidance.
- The aircraft's objective is to stabilise at zero attitude **as soon as
  possible** after an obstacle is cleared.

In short: the leading line is a **flat line at the junction altitude**,
climbing *only* when a terrain safety floor rises above it, and
descending *back* to the junction altitude as fast as the max descent
rate allows once the obstacle is past.

## Junction altitude resolution

Each side of each strip has a "junction" — the point where the leading
line meets the strip body. The altitude at that junction is resolved in
this priority order:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Explicit resolver (AMSL / iAMSL)">
    The caller may pass a `junctionAltitudeResolver` callback. For AMSL
    the resolver returns the strip's flat MSL altitude. For iAMSL it
    returns the per-strip optimised altitude. This keeps the leading line
    anchored at exactly the band's altitude regardless of what the
    terrain does under the extension.
  </Step>
  <Step title="First/last body waypoint (iAGL)">
    When no resolver is passed (the iAGL path), the junction altitude
    comes from the first body waypoint (for the approach side) or the
    last (for the departure side). This means the leading line smoothly
    picks up from wherever the terrain-following profile ended — no step
    discontinuity at the junction.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Strip.altitudeMsl fallback">
    If there are no waypoints, the strip's stored `altitudeMsl` is used.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Zero">
    Last-resort fallback for pathological input.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Sampling the line

Sample count is auto-computed from the same speed-based step iAGL uses:

```text
sample_step (m) = clamp( speed_ms * 0.25, 2.0, 100.0 )   # 4 samples/sec
n               = max( 2, ceil(leading_m / sample_step) + 1 )
dt              = sample_step / speed_ms
max_climb_seg   = max_climb_rate_ms * dt
max_desc_seg    = max_descent_rate_ms * dt
```

Sample positions are laid along the heading direction starting at the
strip junction (strip start for the approach side, strip end for the
departure side) and walking outward. Each position is fed to the
terrain provider to obtain its DEM elevation.

For the approach side, the junction sits at the last sample index; for
the departure side it sits at the first. That junction sample is the
one whose altitude is **locked** during the clamp passes.

## The profile computation

The leading-line profile is structurally similar to the iAGL clamp
but with two critical differences: the target altitude floor is
`max(junctionAlt, terrain + AGL)` instead of just `terrain + AGL`, and
one sample is **pinned** to the junction altitude and never moves.

### 1. Target floor

```text
target[i] = max( junction_alt, terrain[i] + flight_height_agl )
```

This is the "no AGL descent below junction" rule made concrete: the
target starts as a flat line at the junction altitude, and is only
pulled **up** where the terrain safety floor exceeds it. The profile is
not allowed to dive under this target at any sample.

### 2. Initial guess + junction lock

```text
profile = copy(target)
profile[junction_index] = junction_alt
```

The junction sample is fixed to the junction altitude at the start and
is **skipped** inside the clamp loops so it never drifts.

### 3. Forward/backward clamp to a fixed point

```text
for iter in 0..7:
    # Forward pass
    for i in 1..n-1 (skip junction_index):
        lower = profile[i-1] - max_desc_seg
        upper = profile[i-1] + max_climb_seg
        profile[i] = clamp(profile[i], lower, upper)
        profile[i] = max(profile[i], target[i])       # floor wins

    # Backward pass
    for i in n-2..0 (skip junction_index):
        # To reach profile[i+1] in one segment we need
        # profile[i] in [profile[i+1] - max_climb, profile[i+1] + max_desc]
        lower = profile[i+1] - max_climb_seg
        upper = profile[i+1] + max_desc_seg
        profile[i] = clamp(profile[i], lower, upper)
        profile[i] = max(profile[i], target[i])       # floor wins

    if max_delta < 1e-4:
        break
```

The forward pass enforces physics from predecessor to current sample;
the backward pass from successor to current sample. Each pass re-applies
the target floor after clamping so the profile never falls below the
terrain-safety surface *or* below the junction altitude (whichever is
higher at that sample).

Convergence is normally reached in 2–3 iterations; the loop is capped at
8 as a safety net. The epsilon is `1e-4` m.

<Tip>
  Because the junction sample is skipped inside the loop, the profile
  *radiates outward* from the junction anchor: altitudes closer to the
  band are pulled toward the junction altitude, and only the far end
  of the leading line can diverge if a nearby hill forces a climb.
</Tip>

### Practical outcomes

<Steps>
  <Step title="No obstacles in the leading line">
    `terrain + AGL` never exceeds `junction_alt`, so
    `target[i] == junction_alt` everywhere. The entire leading line sits
    at the junction altitude — a perfect flat extension of the strip.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Single hill in the middle">
    `target[i]` bumps up where the hill is. The forward pass propagates
    climb *from* the junction; the backward pass ensures the climb
    *into* the obstacle starts early enough for the physical rate
    limit. Once past the hill, the next clamp pass drives the altitude
    back down toward `junction_alt` at exactly `max_desc_seg` per step,
    hitting the junction altitude again as soon as physics allows. **No
    valley-dive, no over-shoot.**
  </Step>
  <Step title="Steep terrain everywhere">
    `target[i] > junction_alt` for every sample. The profile becomes
    identical to an iAGL profile — the clamp still respects rate limits
    and always stays above the safety floor.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Attachment back to the strip

The resulting altitudes are wrapped into `StripWaypoint` objects with
their `position` and `terrainMsl`, then written into the strip's
`approachWaypoints` and `departureWaypoints` fields via `copyWith`. The
strip body (`waypoints` / `altitudeMsl`) is **not** touched — leading
lines are an additive extension, not a rewrite.

When serpentine ordering runs later, the approach and departure lists
are both reversed **and swapped** on odd-index strips, so "approach"
always denotes the physical entry regardless of flight order. This is
why the approach and departure slots exist as separate fields on
`FlightStrip` rather than being inlined at the ends of the `waypoints`
list.

## Mode matrix

| Mode  | Strip body               | Junction altitude source                    | Leading line shape |
| ----- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------ |
| AMSL  | Flat at `altitudeMsl`    | Explicit: `altitudeMsl`                     | Flat + climb-only terrain avoidance |
| iAMSL | Flat at per-strip median | Explicit: `strip.altitudeMsl` (per strip)   | Flat + climb-only terrain avoidance |
| iAGL  | Dense terrain-follow     | Implicit: first/last body waypoint          | Smooth continuation + climb-only avoidance |
